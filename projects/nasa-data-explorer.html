NASA Data Explorer

An intuitive data exploration tool that makes NASA's vast open datasets accessible to the public through rich visual interfaces, guided discovery flows, and clear explanatory context for non-expert audiences.

Role

UX Designer (Academic Project)

Duration

10 weeks (2023)

Tools

Figma, Observable, D3.js concepts

Type

Web Application

NASA Data Explorer — Cover Image

Overview

NASA provides hundreds of open datasets covering climate, astronomy, earth science, and space exploration. While these datasets are publicly available, they're largely inaccessible to people without scientific or technical backgrounds. This project was born from a simple question: what if anyone could explore space data the way scientists do?

Problem

NASA's existing data portals are designed for researchers, not the public. They require domain knowledge to navigate, use technical jargon, and provide no guided discovery — leaving curious non-experts feeling lost and unable to engage with fascinating scientific data.

  • Portals require prior knowledge to find relevant datasets
  • No contextual explanations or "why this matters" framing
  • Raw data tables without accessible visualization options
  • No guided entry points for exploratory browsing
  • Overwhelming information architecture with 200+ dataset categories
NASA Portal Audit / Heuristic Evaluation

Goals

  • Lower the barrier to entry for non-expert public users
  • Create guided "discovery paths" organized by curiosity and interest
  • Pair data visualizations with plain-language explanations
  • Enable sharing of interesting data stories on social media
  • Remain faithful to the scientific integrity of the underlying data

Process

User Research: Recruited 10 participants across two segments — "space enthusiasts with no science background" and "high school students." Ran contextual inquiry sessions observing how they tried to use NASA's existing portals, then interviewed them about their curiosity and questions.

User Research Synthesis

Opportunity Areas: Three major opportunities emerged: (1) Curiosity-Led Navigation — organizing content around questions people actually ask; (2) Visual-First Exploration — leading with striking visualizations before data tables; (3) Contextual Storytelling — every dataset paired with a narrative hook.

IA & Interaction Design: Designed a card-based browsing interface organized into themes (Earth, Space, Climate, Missions). Each theme had curated "starter stories" to draw users in before offering deeper data exploration.

Information Architecture & Wireflows

Visualization Design: Collaborated with an information design student to sketch and prototype 8 different chart types for key datasets. Ran preference testing to identify which visualizations resonated most with our target audience.

Final Interface — High Fidelity

Outcome

  • Usability test completion rate: 92% (vs. 34% for current NASA portal)
  • Average session length in prototype: 8.3 minutes
  • 8 of 10 participants said they'd return to use it regularly
  • Project presented at university design symposium
  • Selected as a top student project by faculty jury

Reflection

The most valuable lesson from this project was about the power of a good "entry point." Users didn't need all the data — they needed one compelling hook that made them want to explore further. Great design for public data tools is fundamentally about igniting curiosity, not delivering completeness.
