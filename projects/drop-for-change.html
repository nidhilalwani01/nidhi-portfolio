Drop for Change

A mobile-first product concept that connects water-conscious consumers with social impact organizations — turning everyday water-saving habits into measurable charitable donations and community impact.

Role

UX Researcher & Designer

Duration

8 weeks (2022)

Tools

Figma, Miro, Maze

Type

Mobile App Concept

Drop for Change — Cover Image

Overview

Drop for Change began as a volunteer UX research project for a social impact startup with a bold idea: what if conserving water at home could directly fund clean water access for communities in need? The product needed to be simple enough for daily habit formation while emotionally resonant enough to build long-term engagement.

I joined the team as a UX researcher and later expanded my role to product design, contributing to both the concept validation and the initial design direction.

Problem

Most environmental apps fall into one of two traps: they're either too guilt-inducing (which leads to disengagement) or too abstract (which fails to motivate action). Drop for Change needed to thread the needle — making users feel empowered and connected to real impact without being preachy or overwhelming.

  • Users don't know how to connect personal water usage to global impact
  • Habit-forming apps need immediate, tangible rewards to sustain engagement
  • Donation flows are often friction-heavy and trust-deficient
  • People want to help but feel individual action is pointless
  • Existing water apps focus on tracking, not behavior change
Research Findings / Insight Cards

Goals

  • Design a habit loop that makes water saving feel rewarding and meaningful
  • Create a transparent, trust-building donation and impact tracking experience
  • Keep the onboarding under 2 minutes with zero required sign-up
  • Build a social layer that creates community without requiring constant attention
  • Design inclusively — for users across age groups and tech comfort levels

Process

Research: Ran 15 user interviews across two groups — "eco-conscious millennials" and "environmentally curious but not active" users. Ran a diary study with 8 participants over 2 weeks to understand their water habits and emotional relationship with environmental issues.

Diary Study Synthesis

Key Insights: Four insights drove the design: (1) People need proof of impact, not just promises; (2) Gamification works only when tied to real-world stakes; (3) Frictionless giving beats large, infrequent donations; (4) Social accountability is more motivating than personal guilt.

Concept Design: The core mechanic: users log water-saving actions (shorter shower, skipping car wash), earn "drops," and choose how those drops are converted into micro-donations to vetted water organizations. The impact is visualized in real-time — "Your drops = 3 days of clean water for a family."

User Flow & Core Mechanic Design

Visual Design: A calming, water-inspired palette (teals, soft blues, warm whites) with fluid, organic shapes. Avoided dark patterns typical of donation apps. Typography optimized for emotional warmth and readability.

UI Design — Screens & Components

Validation: Ran unmoderated usability tests via Maze with 20 participants. Measured task completion, desirability scores, and Net Promoter Score.

Outcome

  • Concept NPS Score: +62 (industry benchmark for apps: +30)
  • Onboarding task completion rate: 96%
  • 87% of participants said they'd use it daily if available
  • 9 of 20 users spontaneously mentioned sharing it with family
  • Concept presented to two social impact accelerators for potential funding

Reflection

Drop for Change reinforced a belief I hold deeply: the best social impact products don't lecture — they invite. When we shifted our framing from "save water or else" to "your small actions create real stories," everything changed. The design became a vehicle for hope rather than urgency.

I also learned that designing for behavior change requires as much empathy as skill. Understanding why people don't act — not just what would make them act — was the key that unlocked the most effective design decisions.
